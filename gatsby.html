F. Scott Fitzgerald · 1925

The Great Gatsby

Long Island & New York City · Summer 1922 · A novel about the American Dream's seduction and its lie — told by the one person in the room who isn't quite fooled.

Context & Setting
The Jazz Age / The Roaring Twenties
Post-WWI America · 1922
Written in 1925, set in 1922. America after WWI is flush with new money, loosened morals (Prohibition creating speakeasy culture), and a giddy, brittle optimism. The old aristocracy (the Buchanans of East Egg) and the new rich (Gatsby of West Egg) coexist uneasily. Fitzgerald saw through the party: underneath the champagne and the shirts is profound emptiness. The novel is both a celebration and an autopsy of the decade.
East Egg vs. West Egg vs. The Valley of Ashes
Geography as class
East Egg = old money, inherited wealth, careless cruelty. West Egg = new money, gaudy ambition, striving. The Valley of Ashes between them = the working poor, the people the wealthy drive through without seeing — where George and Myrtle Wilson live and die. New York City = freedom from social rules, the place where affairs happen and identities dissolve. These four locations form the novel's entire social map.
Characters — Full Reference
Jay Gatsby — born James Gatz · Protagonist
~30 years old · West Egg · New money from bootlegging
Gatsby is the American Dream in human form — and its hollow center. Born James Gatz to poor farmers in North Dakota, he reinvented himself entirely: new name, new history, new accent, new mansion. His entire fortune and his entire performance of selfhood exist for one purpose: to reclaim Daisy Buchanan, who represents not a person but a world — the world of old money and effortless belonging that rejected him. Fitzgerald makes Gatsby tragic precisely because his dream is not Daisy herself but the idea of her, which means no real Daisy could ever satisfy it. He is both magnificent and deluded. Nick's final judgment — "You're worth the whole damn bunch put together" — is the most complicated compliment in American fiction: true, and also insufficient to save him.
"Can't repeat the past? Why of course you can!" — Gatsby
Nick Carraway — Narrator
29 · Yale · Bond salesman · Gatsby's neighbor
The novel's narrator is its most interesting problem. Nick claims to be one of the few honest people he knows — then participates in covering up Myrtle's murder, enables Gatsby's obsession, and judges everyone around him while being morally complicit in their worst actions. He is from the Midwest (like Fitzgerald), educated at Yale, and cousin to Daisy. His admiration for Gatsby is almost romantic in its intensity. By the end, he has seen through the East entirely and retreats home — but the reader should notice that Nick's narration is retrospective, curated, and not entirely reliable. Ask yourself: whose story is he telling, and why?
"In my younger and more vulnerable years my father gave me some advice that I've been turning over in my mind ever since: 'Whenever you feel like criticizing anyone,' he told me, 'just remember that all the people in this world haven't had the advantages that you've had.'"
Daisy Buchanan née Fay
Tom's wife · Gatsby's dream · Nick's cousin
Daisy is the novel's most debated figure. She is charming, beautiful, careless — and ultimately she lets Gatsby take the blame for killing Myrtle and leaves with Tom the same day. Is she a villain? Fitzgerald suggests she is something more structural: a product of a world that never required her to be accountable for anything. Her voice, Nick says, is "full of money." She doesn't love Gatsby; she loves being loved by him. She chooses safety and privilege over everything, every time, and feels briefly bad about it.
"Her voice is full of money." — Gatsby (via Nick)
Tom Buchanan
Daisy's husband · East Egg · Old money bully
The novel's clearest villain, if it has one. Enormous, athletic, racist (he quotes a eugenicist book with approval), casually unfaithful, and accustomed to absolute entitlement. He destroys Gatsby not because he loves Daisy but because Gatsby represents a challenge to his social order. His class allows him to cheat, to manipulate, to let Gatsby die for Daisy's crime — and face no consequences. He and Daisy are "careless people" who "smashed up things and creatures and then retreated back into their money."
"I suppose the latest thing is to sit back and let Mr. Nobody from Nowhere make love to your wife." — Tom
Jordan Baker
Professional golfer · Nick's love interest
Daisy's friend and Nick's romantic entanglement. Jordan is independent, dishonest (she cheated in a tournament), and clear-eyed about the world she inhabits. She represents a version of Daisy who has agency but uses it selfishly. Nick is drawn to her but ultimately judges her: she's "incurably dishonest" — which is rich, coming from Nick. Their failed relationship mirrors the novel's larger argument about who can actually connect with anyone in this world.
Myrtle Wilson
George's wife · Tom's mistress · Valley of Ashes
The novel's most tragic figure, and its most overlooked. Myrtle is striving — she wants out of the Valley of Ashes and believes Tom is her ticket. She is fleshy, vital, alive in ways Daisy is not. She is killed by Daisy's car (driven by Daisy, though Gatsby takes the blame) in the most brutal image of class violence in American literature: the rich literally running down the poor and driving away. She gets almost no interiority in the novel — another form of her erasure.
George Wilson
Myrtle's husband · Garage owner
Pale, passive, defeated — the Valley of Ashes made human. George is the man the American Dream forgot: he works hard, owns a small business, loves his wife (who despises him for his poverty). When Myrtle dies, Tom redirects his grief toward Gatsby, knowing Gatsby is innocent. George kills Gatsby and then himself. He is used by every powerful person in the novel and discarded. His death completes the pattern: the poor die; the rich move on.
Meyer Wolfsheim
Gatsby's business associate
The man who fixed the 1919 World Series — the novel's signal that Gatsby's money comes from organized crime. Wolfsheim represents the underworld economy that makes self-made men possible; he also refuses to attend Gatsby's funeral, which is one of the novel's bleakest moments. Gatsby built his dream on criminal money and is mourned by almost no one.
Major Symbols — Know These Cold
The Green Light
At the end of Daisy's dock across the bay. Gatsby stares at it at night with outstretched arm. It represents his dream of Daisy — and more broadly the American Dream itself: always visible, always just out of reach, always across the water. After he meets Daisy again, the green light loses some of its power; the dream is always more powerful than its object. The final paragraph connects it to the original settlers seeing America's "fresh, green breast" — the country that was always a dream, always a lie.
The Eyes of Doctor T.J. Eckleburg
A faded billboard over the Valley of Ashes: giant blue eyes behind yellow spectacles, staring down at everything. They represent the eyes of God — or the absence of God: an advertiser's forgotten image now presiding over moral wasteland. George Wilson, mad with grief, says "God sees everything" while looking at the billboard. Wilson mistakes commerce for divinity. Fitzgerald's point: in this America, the two are indistinguishable.
The Valley of Ashes
The industrial wasteland between the Eggs and New York — where poor people live, where Myrtle dies, where George kills and is destroyed. The ashes are literally what remains when the wealthy burn through resources. It is the hidden cost of the parties in West Egg: someone has to live in what those parties produce as waste. Fitzgerald makes the geography moral.
Gatsby's Parties / The Shirts
The parties are all performance — Gatsby doesn't drink at them, barely participates, watches for Daisy. When he throws his shirts at Daisy and she weeps ("I've never seen such beautiful shirts before"), it's one of the novel's most psychologically acute moments: she's crying because they represent the life she chose and the life she abandoned Gatsby for. The shirts are the American Dream made fabric.
Major Themes for Your Exam
The American Dream as Illusion
Gatsby achieves everything the American Dream promises — wealth, reinvention, the great house — and it is entirely hollow. He cannot buy his way into East Egg. He cannot buy Daisy's real love. He cannot buy a past. The novel's final image — boats against the current, borne back ceaselessly into the past — is Fitzgerald's thesis: America is a country that promises forward motion but is actually structured to prevent it, especially for those without the right birth.
Class & Old Money vs. New Money
The novel is obsessed with the difference between earned and inherited wealth. Tom and Daisy's cruelty is enabled by the fact that their money has always been there — they have never had to be accountable to it. Gatsby's money is new, criminal, and insufficient to penetrate the social world he wants. No matter how many parties he throws or how many shirts he owns, he cannot become East Egg.
The Past & Obsession
Gatsby's fatal flaw is his belief that the past can be repeated — that five years can be erased, that Daisy can be retrieved unchanged, that the dream can be rebuilt from scratch. Nick's "Can't repeat the past? Why of course you can!" captures his magnificent delusion. The novel is a tragedy of temporal thinking: Gatsby lives entirely in an imagined past that was probably never as perfect as he remembers it.
Carelessness & Moral Corruption
Fitzgerald's moral indictment of the wealthy is concentrated in one word: careless. Tom and Daisy are "careless people" — they break things and people and retreat into money. No one is held accountable for Myrtle's death; no one attends Gatsby's funeral except Nick and the owl-eyed man. Wealth in this novel is not glamorous — it is a way of escaping consequence.
Identity & Self-Invention
Gatsby/James Gatz is the purest expression of American self-invention. He rewrites himself from poverty to glamour, creates a biography ("Oxford man"), changes his name. But Morrison and Wilson both ask the same question from different angles: can you truly reinvent yourself, or does the self you're fleeing always catch up? Gatsby's invented self is destroyed the moment Tom exposes him as "Mr. Nobody from Nowhere."
Nick's Reliability & Complicity
A sophisticated exam answer will note that Nick is not a neutral narrator. He enables Gatsby's obsession, covers up Daisy's role in Myrtle's death, participates in the affair logistics, then judges everyone. His narration is retrospective and curated. The novel is as much about Nick's infatuation with Gatsby and with a world he claims to reject as it is about Gatsby himself.
Essential Quotes for the Exam
"So we beat on, boats against the current, borne back ceaselessly into the past."— Nick, final line of the novel
"They were careless people, Tom and Daisy — they smashed up things and creatures and then retreated back into their money or their vast carelessness."— Nick
"Can't repeat the past? Why of course you can!"— Gatsby to Nick
"Her voice is full of money."— Gatsby on Daisy, as reported by Nick
Exam Writing Tips — Gatsby
If your prompt is about the American Dream:
Don't just say "Gatsby represents the American Dream." Argue that Fitzgerald uses Gatsby to expose the Dream as structurally deceptive — it promises meritocracy but delivers class rigidity. Use East vs. West Egg, Gatsby's criminally-sourced fortune, and his social rejection despite his wealth. The green light is your best symbol.
If your prompt is about identity or self-invention:
Connect Gatsby's invention of himself to his destruction: the self he built is entirely contingent on Daisy's acceptance. Without her validation, James Gatz has no Jay Gatsby. Compare to Troy in Fences (destroyed by a self built around baseball) and Sula (who builds a self with no social support structure). All three reinvent themselves; all three are destroyed by what they built themselves around.
