August Wilson · 1985 · Pittsburgh Cycle, Play 6

Fences

Pittsburgh's Hill District · 1957–1965 · A play about the violence of unfulfilled dreams, Black fatherhood, and the fences men build to keep things in — and out.

Context & Setting
The Hill District, Pittsburgh
1957 — The year of racial integration in baseball
Set in a small yard in Pittsburgh's predominantly Black Hill District, a neighborhood that was simultaneously vibrant community and a cage — bounded by poverty, racism, and limited mobility. 1957 is deliberate: Jackie Robinson integrated baseball in 1947, and the major leagues are slowly, resentfully opening to Black players. Troy Maxson came a generation too early. The drama lives in that gap: what it costs to have been exactly on the wrong side of history.
August Wilson's Pittsburgh Cycle
The larger project
Fences is the sixth play in Wilson's decade-by-decade chronicle of Black American life in the 20th century. Wilson wanted to give Black Americans the same kind of epic, specific, particular history that white American theater had always claimed for itself. Each play is set in Pittsburgh's Hill District, each in a different decade. Fences covers the 1950s — the decade of the Great Migration's second wave, suburbanization (which excluded Black families), and the early Civil Rights movement.
Characters — Full Reference
Troy Maxson — Protagonist
53 years old · Garbage collector · Former Negro League baseball player
The most complex character August Wilson ever wrote. Troy was a genuinely gifted baseball player — possibly one of the best in the country — but played in the Negro Leagues because integration hadn't happened yet. By the time it did, he was too old. That injustice is real and devastating. But Wilson refuses to make Troy simply a victim: he is also a man who cheats on his wife, drives his son away, brings home an illegitimate child, and prevents Cory from pursuing a football scholarship he himself couldn't have. Troy repeats his father's damage. He is enormous and flawed and tragic in the classical sense — his greatness and his destruction come from the same source: an absolute refusal to be diminished, which makes him diminish everyone around him.
"I had me a chance once. You got yours coming. The time ain't now." — Troy to Cory
Rose Maxson
Troy's wife · The moral center
Rose is the play's true moral conscience — patient, clear-eyed, and ultimately devastating when her patience runs out. She has built her entire life inside the fence of her marriage to Troy, accepting his limitations and protecting his ego. When Troy reveals Alberta's pregnancy, Rose's response is one of the most powerful moments in American drama: she agrees to raise the baby, but tells Troy he is now a womanless man. She chooses the child (Raynell) but closes the door on Troy completely. Her fence protects her now; his cannot reach her.
"I been standing with you! I been right here with you, Troy. I got a life too. I gave eighteen years of my life to stand in the same spot with you." — Rose
Cory Maxson
Troy's son · 17 · Football recruit
A college football recruiter has come to scout Cory — the same opportunity Troy never had, in a different sport in a (slightly) more open era. Troy forbids him from pursuing it, ostensibly to protect him from disappointment, but really because he cannot bear watching his son succeed where he failed, or become something Troy cannot understand. The father-son dynamic is the play's engine: Troy's destruction of Cory's dream is the clearest expression of how trauma passes between generations. By the end, Cory has enlisted in the Marines and returns for Troy's funeral still unreconciled.
"You ain't never gave me nothing! You ain't never done nothing but hold me back." — Cory to Troy
Jim Bono
Troy's best friend · Witness
Troy's oldest friend — met in prison. Bono is the play's audience surrogate: he admires Troy, knows him completely, and watches with increasing unease as Troy's choices destroy his family. He is the one person Troy is honest with (to a point). His friendship with Troy cools after the Alberta revelation — not out of judgment but because he can't watch. Bono also represents the idea that a man can live decently within limitations; his marriage to Lucille is not spectacular but it is faithful.
Lyons Maxson
Troy's son from first marriage · Musician
Troy's oldest son, from a relationship before Rose. Lyons is a jazz musician — which Troy cannot respect, because it doesn't represent security or hard work in Troy's terms. Lyons comes around mainly to borrow money, which Troy resents but gives. Their relationship is warmer than Troy and Cory's precisely because Lyons has already given up on his father's approval. He appears at the end of the play having served time in prison.
Gabriel Maxson
Troy's brother · Brain-damaged veteran
Returned from WWII with a metal plate in his head, believing he is the Archangel Gabriel. He carries a trumpet and believes he is preparing to open the gates of heaven for Saint Peter. Troy used Gabriel's veteran's disability payment to buy the house the family lives in — a moral compromise he lives with. Gabriel is the play's spiritual register: his madness is also vision, and when he blows his trumpet at the end (it makes no sound), then dances — the gates of heaven open. It's one of the most powerful stage directions in American drama.
"That's the way that go!" — Gabriel, after his dance at Troy's funeral
Raynell Maxson
Troy's daughter by Alberta · Infant → age 7
Alberta dies in childbirth. Rose agrees to raise Raynell — "I'll take care of your baby for you... 'cause, like you say, she's innocent... and you can't visit the sins of the father upon the child." Raynell appears at the play's end, seven years later. She is the garden Rose tends; she is also what Troy left behind. Her duet with Cory on the old song connects them: children of Troy's love and damage, still singing.
The Fence — Symbol Analysis
What does the fence mean?

Rose's fence: She asks Troy to build it. For Rose, the fence keeps the family together — it is love made physical, an act of holding. She wants to enclose what matters before the world takes it.

Troy's fence: Troy resists building it. For Troy, fences are what the world builds around him — barriers that kept him out of the major leagues, out of opportunity, out of the American dream. He doesn't want to build his own cage.

Bono's reading: "Some people build fences to keep people out... and other people build fences to keep people in." This is the play's thesis line. The same fence means opposite things depending on who built it and why.

The irony: Troy finishes the fence the day he tells Rose about Alberta. He builds it — finally, completely — and immediately destroys what it was meant to protect. The completed fence marks the end of the marriage.

Major Themes for Your Exam
The American Dream & Race
Troy is the American Dream's nightmare: a man with the talent, work ethic, and drive the dream demands — who was still excluded by race. His bitterness is the bitterness of someone who played by rules that were rigged. Wilson's point: the American Dream is not a universal promise. For Black Americans in the 1950s, it was a myth that demanded belief while systematically denying access.
Generational Trauma
Troy's father was brutal and abandoning. Troy learned that survival required hardness. He passes this to Cory — not because he doesn't love his son, but because he cannot imagine love that doesn't look like endurance. The fence Wilson is really writing about is the one men build around their own hearts, and then mistake for protection.
Death as Constant Presence
Troy speaks of Death as a personal adversary — "I wrestled with Death for three days and three nights" (his illness during a stint in prison). He personifies it, challenges it, makes it into a wrestling match he believes he won. This is Troy's coping mechanism for a life that has nearly killed him multiple times. But Death comes for him quietly in the yard, doing his exercises.
Loyalty, Betrayal & Infidelity
Alberta represents the only place Troy allows himself tenderness and escape from responsibility. This is not a defense of his infidelity — Wilson doesn't make it one. But it is an explanation: Troy cannot be small with Rose; he cannot be weak; she holds him accountable in ways Alberta does not. His betrayal of Rose is real, devastating, and explicable without being excusable.
Sports as Metaphor
Baseball runs through the play as metaphor and missed life. Troy uses baseball language constantly — "full count," "three strikes," "got to take the crookeds with the straights." Sport was the arena where America tested its myths of meritocracy; it was also one of the last to integrate, and the integration (which Troy calls out) came too late for him. His inability to see Cory's football as opportunity mirrors his inability to see beyond his own wound.
What a Father Owes a Son
Troy believes he owes Cory food, shelter, and a chance to work — not love, not possibility, not support for dreams. "A man ain't supposed to like his father." He provides for Cory in the way he was taught provision works, without recognizing that provision and love are not the same thing. This is the play's saddest gap: Troy loves Cory and has no language for it that doesn't come out as control.
Essential Quotes for the Exam
"Some people build fences to keep people out... and other people build fences to keep people in. Rose wants to hold on to you all. She loves you."— Bono to Cory
"I don't know what got into me. Alberta... she gives me a different idea... a different understanding about myself. I can step out of this house and get away from the pressures and problems... be a different man."— Troy to Rose, confessing the affair
"Death ain't nothing but a fastball on the outside corner."— Troy
Exam Writing Tips — Fences
If your prompt is about the American Dream:
Argue that Wilson uses Troy to show the Dream is not race-neutral. Troy has everything the Dream requires except access — then gets blamed for the bitterness his exclusion produces. Connect to Cory: the question isn't whether dreams are possible, it's whether the father's wound becomes the son's wall.
If your prompt is about the fence symbol:
Track who wants it built and why. Rose's fence = love/protection. Troy's resistance = refusal of cages. The irony of Troy completing it the day he betrays Rose. Gabriel's trumpet at the end opening heaven's gates = the fence that truly matters is spiritual. Use Bono's quote as your thesis line.
