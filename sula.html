Toni Morrison · 1973

Sula

The Bottom, Medallion, Ohio · 1919–1965 · A novel about female selfhood, community, and the cost of freedom.

Context & Setting
The Bottom
Place as irony
A Black neighborhood in the hills above Medallion, Ohio — named with a dark joke. A white farmer tricked a freed slave into accepting hillside land by calling it "the bottom of heaven." This founding lie runs through everything: Black life in Medallion is built on deception and displacement. By the novel's end, the Bottom is torn down for a golf course — erased by the same white world that created it.
Historical Frame
WWI to 1965
The novel spans from WWI (which traumatizes Shadrack) to 1965 (the year after the Civil Rights Act). Morrison shows how national history — war, the Great Migration, Jim Crow, urban development — shapes private lives in a small Black community. The New River Road bridge that Jude can't get hired to build is shorthand for systemic racism blocking Black economic life.
Family Tree — The Peace Family
Eva Peace — Matriarch (Generation 1)
Abandoned by BoyBoy · Runs a boarding house
The terrifying center of the Peace family. Abandoned by her husband BoyBoy with three small children and no money, Eva likely sacrificed her own leg under a train for the insurance payout — then returned 18 months later with enough money to build a house and never discuss it. She rules her household with absolute authority. Her love is real but devastating: she burns her son Plum alive to end his suffering, and watches Hannah burn in the backyard without moving to save her. Eva accuses Sula of doing the same — of having that cold, watching eye inherited from her.
Key quote: "You don't love nobody but yourself." — Eva to Sula
Pearl Peace (Generation 2)
Eva's eldest daughter
Marries young at 14 and moves to Flint, Michigan. Nearly disappears from the novel entirely. Her departure represents one path: escape through conventional marriage. Morrison gives her almost no interiority, which is itself a comment on how women who choose conformity early are erased from the story.
Hannah Peace (Generation 2)
Sula's mother · Eva's middle child
Warm, sensual, and completely free with men — she sleeps with the husbands of neighbors she knows without guilt or attachment. Unlike Sula, her sexuality is accepted by the community because she is non-threatening: she doesn't want anything from the men except the act itself. She loves Sula genuinely but once tells a friend she doesn't like her — overheard by Sula as a child, a wound that never closes. Dies horribly: her dress catches fire in the backyard while Eva watches from an upstairs window. Eva claims she jumped to save her; neighbors say she didn't move.
"She was an easy woman... she didn't need anything from you."
Plum (Ralph) Peace (Generation 2)
Eva's youngest · WWI veteran
Eva's most beloved child. Returns from WWI a heroin addict, regressing into helplessness. Eva sets him on fire in his sleep — she frames it as mercy: she would rather kill him than watch him crawl back into her womb. It's one of the novel's most disturbing scenes, and one of its most honest: Eva's love has always been about possession and control, not freedom.
"I held him close first... for a long time." — Eva on killing Plum
Sula Peace (Generation 3) — Protagonist
Hannah's daughter · Born with a birthmark over one eye
The novel's fierce, uncompromising center. Her birthmark is read differently by everyone: a rose, a snake, a stemmed rose, Hannah's ashes, evil itself — Morrison uses it to show how a community projects its fears onto a woman who refuses to be defined. Sula grows up witnessing Eva's violence and Hannah's sensuality; she absorbs both. At 12, she watches Chicken Little drown and says nothing. At 17, she cuts off her fingertip with a knife to warn neighborhood boys not to mess with her. She leaves the Bottom at 18, returns at 27 transformed and entirely herself. She sleeps with Nel's husband Jude — not out of cruelty or desire, but almost carelessly, because she cannot imagine that it matters more than her own freedom. The community calls her evil; Morrison asks: by whose standard? She dies at 30, alone, and the novel mourns her and doesn't.
"I got my mind. And what goes on in it. Which is to say, I got me." — Sula
Family Tree — The Wright Family
Origin note
Helene was born to Rochelle, a Creole prostitute in New Orleans, and raised strictly by her grandmother Cecile Sabat. She reinvented herself utterly — becoming the most proper woman in the Bottom — as a total rejection of her mother. Everything about her identity is constructed to flee where she came from.
Helene Wright née Sabat · (Generation 1)
Matriarch · Obsessively respectable
Nel's mother and the moral opposite of Eva Peace. Where Eva is raw, Helene is polished; where Eva destroys from love, Helene suffocates from decorum. She raises Nel with such rigid expectations of propriety that Nel enters adulthood without a self. The pivotal scene: on a train south, a white conductor humiliates Helene and she responds with a smile — a submissive, accommodating smile that makes the Black soldiers in the car look away in shame. Nel witnesses this and decides, silently, not to become her mother. Then she does anyway.
Wiley Wright
Helene's husband · Railroad worker
Largely absent from the household — works the railroad. His presence is felt mainly as a reason Helene maintains the house in a certain way. He is not developed as a character, which is part of Morrison's point: the men in these women's lives are often peripheral to the real drama.
Nel Wright (Generation 2) — Co-protagonist
Sula's best friend · Marries Jude Greene
Nel is everything Sula is not — and the novel insists this is not a hierarchy. Where Sula chooses radical selfhood, Nel chooses community. She marries Jude, keeps house, raises children, stays in Medallion. For years she thinks she is the moral one. When Jude leaves her for Sula, she grieves but doesn't examine her own culpability (she too watched Chicken Little drown without speaking). Near the novel's end, after decades of calling her grief for Jude, she realizes: she was grieving Sula all along. The final line belongs to her — "girl, girl, girlgirlgirl" — the most devastating sentence Morrison wrote.
"It was a fine cry — loud and long — but it had no bottom and it had no top, just circles and circles of sorrow." — Nel at the end
Other Key Characters
Shadrack
WWI veteran · Founder of National Suicide Day
Opens and closes the novel. Shell-shocked by war, he returns to the Bottom unable to control his own hands and terrified of random death. His solution: designate January 3rd as National Suicide Day, a day to face death collectively so it loses its randomness the rest of the year. The community fears him, then absorbs the ritual. He and Sula share a wordless bond — she is the only person he has ever loved. On the final National Suicide Day, after Sula's death, the crowd he leads falls into a flooded tunnel and drowns. He survives, alone.
"If you organized it, if you made it a day, then it loose its teeth."
Jude Greene
Nel's husband · Catalyst for rupture
Marries Nel not from love but from a need for a witness — a woman whose need for him would confirm his own identity, damaged by racism that bars him from work on the bridge. His affair with Sula is almost incidental; he leaves Nel and vanishes. Morrison makes him pathetic rather than villainous. His real function is to reveal what Nel has built her identity on: not love, but usefulness.
Ajax (Albert Jacks)
Sula's one serious lover
The only man who interests Sula because he is genuinely free — rootless, interesting, treats her as an equal rather than a mystery to solve or a body to use. When Sula starts imagining a future (she learns his real name; she cleans the house), Ajax recognizes the trap of domesticity closing and leaves immediately. He represents the freedom Sula values and cannot hold without destroying it through attachment.
Chicken Little
Young boy · The central crime
A neighborhood child accidentally killed when Sula swings him by his hands at the riverbank and he flies into the water. Both Sula and Nel watch him sink and say nothing. The guilt bonds them; the silence condemns them both. Morrison plants this so that decades later, when Nel finally confronts it, we realize: the "good" one was just as guilty as the "evil" one. The crime is shared; only one wears the label.
The Deweys
Eva's three boarders · Comic tragedy
Three unrelated boys Eva takes in and names Dewey — all of them. They merge into one entity over time, indistinguishable even to each other, remaining childlike and stunted forever. Morrison uses them to explore how naming and community expectation can collapse individuality entirely. They are also darkly funny, which is rare in this novel.
BoyBoy
Eva's husband · Absent catalyst
Leaves Eva with three children and no money, then returns years later — prosperous, with another woman — to taunt her with what he's become. Eva hates him calmly and forever. His abandonment is the original wound of the Peace family: it forces Eva to destroy her body to survive, and that act of survival becomes the source of her authority and her violence.
Major Themes for Your Exam
Good vs. Evil — Who Decides?
Morrison's central provocation: the community calls Sula evil, but Nel (the "good" one) also watched Chicken Little drown. Eva kills her son and is called a mother; Sula puts Eva in a nursing home and is called a monster. The novel argues that "good" and "evil" are social designations, not moral facts — assigned to women based on how well they serve the community's needs.
Female Selfhood & Freedom
Sula is Morrison's portrait of a Black woman who refuses every available role — wife, mother, respectable neighbor — and tries to create herself from nothing. The tragedy isn't that she fails. It's that there is nowhere for such a self to live. Her freedom has no social form to take; she can only be read as destruction.
Community as Double-Edged
The Bottom needs Sula to function: her presence makes neighbors love each other more, guard their husbands better, feel morally superior. When she dies, the community loses its scapegoat and its cohesion. The final tunnel disaster is their self-destruction without her to hold them together. Community here is both shelter and trap.
Friendship as the Central Relationship
Morrison places the Sula–Nel friendship above any romance in the novel. They are each other's mirrors and completions. The novel is, at its core, a love story between two women — not romantic, but total. Nel's grief at the end, when she realizes she was mourning Sula rather than Jude all those years, is the novel's thesis: this friendship was the most important thing either of them had.
Race, Place & the Bottom's Destruction
The Bottom's founding (a trick on a freed slave) and eventual destruction (for a golf course) frame the entire novel. Black community in America is always built on stolen or tricked-out ground, and always vulnerable to erasure. The Bottom disappearing is not background — it's the point.
Motherhood Without Redemption
Every mother in the novel fails, on some level: Eva burns her son, Hannah admits she doesn't like her daughter, Helene suffocates Nel's identity, Sula never becomes one. Morrison refuses to let motherhood be the thing that saves or redeems women. It is just another role, and roles are not the same as selves.
Essential Quotes for the Exam
"She was completely free of ambition, with no affection for money, property or things, no greed, no desire to command attention or compliments — no ego."— Narrator, on Sula
"It was a fine cry — loud and long — but it had no bottom and it had no top, just circles and circles of sorrow."— Narrator, on Nel's grief in the final pages
"Being good to somebody is just like being mean to somebody. Risky. You don't get nothing for it."— Sula to Nel
Exam Writing Tips — Sula
If your prompt is about good vs. evil:
Argue that Morrison deliberately refuses to assign good or evil cleanly. Use Chicken Little (both girls watch), Eva/Plum (murder framed as love), and the community's scapegoating of Sula to show that "evil" is a social label, not a moral fact.
If your prompt is about identity/freedom:
Contrast Sula and Nel as two responses to the same problem: how does a Black woman construct a self in a world that only offers her roles? Sula chooses radical selfhood at the cost of belonging; Nel chooses belonging at the cost of selfhood. Neither survives whole.
